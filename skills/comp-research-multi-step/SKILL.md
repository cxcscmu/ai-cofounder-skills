Here are the 14 types of Competitors:

1. **Direct competitors** — 5-7 firms chasing the same core customer/job with a similar product, price point, and go-to-market approach.
2. **Indirect competitors** — 3-5 firms solving the same problem through a fundamentally different model (e.g. partnerships, services, AI-native, community-based).
3. **Analogues** — 3 adjacent-sector players whose moves foreshadow threats or opportunities that {{COMPANY}} could learn from or defend against.
4. **Emergent competitors** — Startups or stealth-mode companies with low visibility now but breakout potential due to backing, IP, or early momentum.
5. **Customer-integrated threats** — Large customers or partners that may develop in-house alternatives, reducing or replacing dependence on {{COMPANY}}.
6. **Free-rider enablers** — Ecosystem participants who benefit from {{COMPANY}}'s platform, API, data, or distribution without creating defensible value in return.
7. **Narrative competitors** — Companies winning investor attention, media cycles, and customer perception — even if their actual offering is weaker.
8. **Geo-localized attackers** — Players dominating a niche region or regulated market, quietly gaining traction before expanding globally.
9. **Cultural competitors** — Companies that act as talent magnets due to mission, leadership, or internal culture — posing a long-term strategic threat.
10. **Compliance catalysts** — Rivals winning business by being first or best at navigating complex legal, regulatory, or industry compliance requirements.
11. **Tech infrastructure substitutes** — Vendors that don't replace the whole solution, but substitute part of {{COMPANY}}'s tech stack (e.g. API layer, workflow engine).
12. **Bundlers / aggregators** — Companies bundling {{COMPANY}}'s value proposition into broader offerings that diminish brand recognition or margin.
13. **Open-source undercutters** — Community-driven or free alternatives that replicate much of the functionality, particularly appealing to technical audiences.
14. **Mission-driven attractors** — Nonprofits, B Corps, or social ventures that win over customers based on values, ethics, or public benefit, even if feature-poor.
